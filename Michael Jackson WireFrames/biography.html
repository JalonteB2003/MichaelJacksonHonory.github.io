Birth

Michael Jackson, also known as Michael Joseph Jackson or Michael Joe Jackson, was born on August 29, 1958, in Gary, Indiana. Hailing from Gary, Indiana, he belonged to one of the most renowned musical families of the rock era.

Jackson 5

In the early 1960s, Joseph Jackson nurtured his sons into a musical ensemble that would eventually gain fame as the Jackson 5. Initially, the Jackson Family group comprised Joseph's elder sons, Tito, Jermaine, and Jackie. Michael Jackson entered the fold at the tender age of 5, swiftly establishing himself as the group's lead vocalist. Despite his youth, he displayed an extraordinary vocal range and emotional depth, astonishing audiences with his ability to convey intricate feelings. The group officially became the Jackson 5 when their older brother Marlon joined their ranks.

The Jackson siblings dedicated countless hours to perfecting their performance. Initially, the Jackson 5 began by playing gigs in their local area, gradually amassing a dedicated fan base. They even independently recorded a single, "Big Boy," featuring "You've Changed" on the B-side, but unfortunately, the record failed to garner significant interest. Undeterred, the group transitioned into becoming the opening act for notable R&B artists like Gladys Knight and The Pips, James Brown, and Sam and Dave. Many of these artists were signed to the renowned Motown record label, and it wasn't long before the Jackson 5 captured the attention of Motown founder, Berry Gordy.

Impressed by the group's talents, Berry Gordy extended a contract to the Jackson 5 on his label in early 1969. Michael Jackson and his brothers relocated to Los Angeles, where they initially resided with Berry Gordy and Diana Ross of the Supremes as they settled into their new surroundings. The Jackson 5 marked their first television appearance during the 1969 Miss Black American Pageant, captivating the audience with a rendition of "It's Your Thing." Their inaugural album, titled "Diana Ross Presents the Jackson 5," graced the charts in December 1969, with the single "I Want You Back" swiftly climbing to the No. 1 spot on the Billboard Hot 100 chart.

A string of chart-topping singles ensued, including "ABC," "The Love You Save," and "I'll Be There." Michael Jackson and the Jackson 5 maintained a demanding schedule of tours and recordings for several years, closely guided by Berry Gordy and the Motown team. Their popularity soared to the extent that they even had their own animated television show, simply titled "The Jackson 5," which aired from 1971 to 1972. Michael Jackson's iconic "robot dance" gained immense popularity after he showcased the move during a performance of the song "Dancing Machine" on The Mike Douglas Show in 1973.

However, beneath the group's remarkable success, tensions brewed behind the scenes. Disagreements arose between Berry Gordy and Joseph Jackson regarding the management of the Jackson children's careers, as the Jacksons sought more creative control over their work. In 1976, the group officially parted ways with Motown, with the exception of Jermaine, who continued to pursue his solo career under the label. Now rebranded as "The Jacksons," the group secured a fresh recording contract with Epic Records. By the release of their third album for Epic, titled "Destiny" in 1978, the brothers had blossomed as talented songwriters.

The King of Pop

Michael Jackson's first solo venture with Epic Records, "Off the Wall" (1979), far surpassed expectations and claimed the title of the best-selling album of the year, ultimately selling over 20 million copies. Helmed by the esteemed producer Quincy Jones, "Off the Wall" spawned two massive international hits, "Don't Stop 'til You Get Enough" and "Rock with You." These songs highlighted Michael's dynamic style and tapped into the disco dance trend of the time.

Three years later, he joined forces with Quincy Jones once again for "Thriller," a tour de force that featured an array of guest stars and catapulted him to global superstardom. "Thriller" not only garnered a multitude of awards, including a record-setting eight Grammy Awards, but it also dominated the charts for over two years and became the best-selling album in history, surpassing 40 million copies sold. The album's first single, "The Girl Is Mine," a mellow duet with Paul McCartney, ascended to number one on the rhythm-and-blues charts and reached number two on the pop charts in the fall of 1982. The follow-up single, "Billie Jean," an electrifying dance track featuring Michael's signature "moonwalk" dance, claimed the top spot on the pop charts, as did "Beat It," which boasted a blistering guitar solo from the renowned Eddie Van Halen. "Beat It" also played a pivotal role in breaking down artificial barriers between Black and white artists on the radio and within the emerging format of music videos on television.

By 1984, Michael Jackson had earned the title of the "King of Pop" worldwide. His much-anticipated Victory reunion tour with his brothers became one of the most popular concert events of 1984. In 1985, Jackson, along with Lionel Richie, co-wrote "We Are the World," the signature single for USA for Africa, an all-star project aimed at famine relief. Further solo albums, including "Bad" (1987), which produced five chart-topping hits (including the title track and "Man in the Mirror"), and "Dangerous" (1991), much of which was produced by the New Jack Swing sensation Teddy Riley, solidified Jackson's dominance in the world of pop music. In 2001, he was inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame, while the Jackson 5 received the same honor in 1997.

Accusations, Financial Diffucilties, and Death

In the early 1990s, Michael Jackson's eccentric and reclusive lifestyle drew increasing controversy. His reputation took a severe hit in 1993 when he faced allegations of child molestation from a 13-year-old boy he had befriended, leading to a civil lawsuit that was settled out of court. In 1994, Jackson secretly wed Lisa Marie Presley, the daughter of Elvis Presley, but their marriage lasted less than two years. Shortly thereafter, he entered into another marriage that resulted in the birth of children, although this union also ended in divorce. Despite his ongoing international celebrity, his image in the United States struggled to recover, and it faced another blow in November 2003 when Jackson was arrested and charged with child molestation.

During the ensuing trial, actor Macaulay Culkin testified in Jackson's defense, as he had met and befriended Jackson at the age of nine when Jackson was an adult. Culkin stated that their bond was rooted in shared experiences of dealing with fame at a young age and having overbearing fathers. He maintained that he had never experienced anything inappropriate with Jackson. Following a 14-week trial that became a media sensation, Jackson was acquitted in 2005.

In the aftermath of these events, Jackson experienced a financial crisis, leading to the sale of many of his significant assets, including his opulent Neverland ranch. He was gearing up for a series of high-profile concerts in hopes of making a comeback when he tragically succumbed to cardiac arrest on June 25, 2009. This event prompted an outpouring of grief from his fans and culminated in a memorial celebration of his life and legacy on July 7 at the Staples Center in Los Angeles. The memorial featured tributes from friends and luminaries, including Stevie Wonder, Berry Gordy, Jr., Brooke Shields, and Al Sharpton.

In August 2009, the coroner ruled Jackson's death a homicide, with the cause being a lethal combination of sedatives and propofol, an anesthetic. In November 2011, Jackson's personal physician was found guilty of involuntary manslaughter in connection with his passing.
